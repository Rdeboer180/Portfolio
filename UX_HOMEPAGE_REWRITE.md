## ABOUT SECTION

### Headline
**"How I Got Here"**

### Copy (Rewritten)
"I started as a web designer in 2010, back when the role encompassed everything—UX, UI, front-end, systems thinking. That wasn't a limitation. It was an education.

Over 14 years, I've built responsive systems, shipped high-traffic ecommerce experiences, and collaborated with engineers who forced me to think in constraints. I learned early that the most beautiful design fails if it doesn't account for scale, shipping, and the real people building it.

From 2021–2025, I operated directly under a Lead UX Manager at Tire Rack. I moved away from solo execution and into the harder work: translating ambiguous UX challenges into production-ready systems, mentoring junior designers on strategic thinking, and serving as the connective tissue between design and engineering. I was trusted with complex problems that had no obvious answers—and learning to live in that ambiguity is what shaped how I approach design today.

During my recent sabbatical, I rebuilt this portfolio entirely using AI-native workflows (Claude, Cursor, Figma Make). Not because I'm chasing trends. Because I'm genuinely curious about what's possible when you pair human judgment with AI-accelerated exploration. I think that's the future of design work—and it excites me.

I'm looking to return to high-impact product work. To problems that matter. To teams that value someone who can see the full picture: the UX, the engineering, the systems, the shipping."

### Key Structural Elements
- Establishes credibility without credential-obsessing
- Explains the UX manager role (translator, mentor, bridge)
- Shows sustained growth from 2010–2025
- Acknowledges the sabbatical and AI learning openly
- Ends with a clear signal: "I'm ready to return to impact"

## ABOUT MY WORK

### Section Headline
**"Problems I Solve"**

### Copy Framework for Case Studies
Each project should follow this structure:

**THE PROBLEM**
What was ambiguous? What was failing? What constraints existed?

**THE SYSTEM**
How did I reframe this as a systems problem? What was the underlying issue? How did I collaborate with engineering?

**THE OUTCOME**
What shipped? How did it scale? What metrics or feedback validated the approach?

### Example Project Reframe

**Current (Generic):**
"I designed a design system for Tire Rack's modernization initiative."

**Rewritten (Strategic):**
"Tire Rack's legacy platform had 200+ components scattered across four systems. Engineering wanted consistency; leadership wanted scalability; customers needed performance. I built a system from scratch that would let a team of engineers implement 50+ new components per sprint without design bottlenecks. The result: unified component language across React + AEM, 40% faster implementation cycles, and a foundation that scaled to 10+ partner brands."

## HERO ANIMATION

### Recommended Progression
```
Layer 1: "Product Designer"
Layer 2: "Design Engineer" 
Layer 3: "Designs What Ships" (final state, emphasized)
```

**Rationale:**
- Layer 1 establishes the primary identity
- Layer 2 adds the technical empathy that differentiates you
- Layer 3 is the resolution—the outcome of combining both

**Alternative Option:**
```
Layer 1: "Systems Thinker"
Layer 2: "Design Engineer"
Layer 3: "Builds With Engineers" (final state)
```

### Animation Behavior
- The final state should *feel* like the answer to the question "Who is this person?"
- No fourth layer (avoid the "lists three unrelated things" feeling)
- Final state should be visually emphasized (size, weight, color, or highlight)

## TONE GUIDELINES

### DO ✓
- Be specific. "I translate ambiguous UX problems into production-ready systems" > "I solve complex problems"
- Show constraints. "Design for shipping" > "Design beautifully"
- Use active voice. "I bridge" > "I am a bridge"
- Lead with outcomes. "This scaled to 10+ teams" > "I built a component library"
- Be confident. "I know how to ship systems" > "I'm passionate about design systems"

### DON'T ✗
- Use filler words: "passionate," "creative," "innovative," "synergy"
- Claim expertise you don't have
- Make it about aesthetics first (your work IS beautiful, but that's not why you're valuable)
- List tools without explaining what they enable
- Sound like a generic portfolio (every designer says they're a "problem solver")
